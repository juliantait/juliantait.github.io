  • The Unprotected Majority: How Identity Inference Silences Mainstream Opinions

    Keywords: identity inference self-censorship stereotypes expressive utility silence

    Experiment Theory

    When an opinion is more common in one group than another, voicing it reveals more than the opinion itself – listeners can draw inferences about the speaker's identity. This project develops an identity-inference theory of self-censorship in which individuals weigh the expressive value of speaking their mind against the identity inferences their statement invites, and stay silent in equilibrium when the latter dominate. Because these inferences are driven by how representative an opinion is of each group rather than by how many people hold it, even mainstream, majority-held opinions can be systematically silenced. The model allows for heterogeneity in expressive values and inference sensitivity, and accommodates stereotypical over-inference in the spirit of Bordalo et al. (2016). A laboratory experiment tests the theory with visual populations of groups and traits, in abstract and political framings, varying whether the opinion distribution is symmetric across groups or representative of the majority or minority group.

  • The Honesty Penalty: Hiding Mistakes in Teams

    with R. Sloof & J. van de Ven

    Keywords: organizational failures blame experiment mistakes lying

    Experiment

    Organisations benefit from the timely disclosure of mistakes, yet workers often conceal their errors. We examine the role of blame avoidance, hypothesizing that mistakes associated with a larger loss in potential earnings increase blame and discourage disclosure. This creates a paradox: mistakes are concealed precisely when concealment is most costly to the organization. We formalize this argument in a model and test it in a vignette study and two incentivized laboratory experiments. The vignette study supports the proposed mechanism, showing that blame increases with the loss in potential earnings. In the laboratory experiments, larger losses do not reduce disclosure, but we do document an important honesty penalty in team settings: the desire to avoid blame induces free-riding by leaving the disclosure of mistakes up to their team members. This pattern emerges both when new teams are formed after each interaction and when teams stay together.

  • Perceived Value-for-Money: An Experiment on Advice-Giving

    with R. Sloof & J. van de Ven

    Keywords: advice-giving value-for-money reciprocity flat fees sunk cost

    Experiment

    Consulting is a lucrative business, yet little is known about how fees shape advisory incentives. Higher fees should elicit greater effort, but they may also create pressure to signal value – potentially distorting advice rather than improving it. We investigate this in a pre-registered experiment with 295 client–advisor pairs, in which advisors are randomly assigned a low or high flat fee before deciding whether to provide advice on their client's estimates in a dot-counting task. Contrary to the signalling hypothesis, we find that higher fees affect neither the frequency, boldness, nor quality of advice – suggesting that fees do not exacerbate advisors' desire to signal perceived value for money. Clients, however, comply more with expensive advice, prefer advisors who advise more frequently, and sanction those who suggest large changes from the status quo.

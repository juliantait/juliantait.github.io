The Honesty Penalty

Hiding mistakes in teams – why does silence pay?

Julian Tait
All projects
The Honesty Penalty
Overview Results Model
Hypothesis The costlier the mistake, the more it should be hidden.

The expectation, following theories of blame in which anger scales with the earnings an action destroys: the bigger the payoff a mistake wipes out, the more the manager blames the worker, and the harder the worker is punished for owning up. A worker who has slipped up faces a trade-off. Hiding the mistake forfeits the recovery the manager could make by switching to the backup project, a cost spread over the whole team; admitting it invites blame, a cost borne alone. Only the blame side grows with the stakes, so raising the payoff of success should tip the trade-off towards silence: harsher punishment of reporters, and fewer mistakes disclosed (Proposition 1 on the model page). The uncomfortable implication the experiments were built to test is that concealment should be most common precisely when disclosure is most valuable.

Results Only hypothetical stakes ever mattered.

In a hypothetical vignette the prediction held: the same disclosed mistake drew more blame and a $248 deeper bonus cut when more money was at stake. In the lab, with real money and real teammates – twice, at very different intensities – the stakes moved nothing: workers hid a third of their mistakes in Experiment 1 and 43% in Experiment 2 whether success paid little or a lot, and punishment did not scale either. What the lab did expose is an honesty penalty: owning up costs a worker about €0.14 extra punishment, and a sole discloser is punished roughly six times more than the silent teammate beside them (€0.54 vs €0.09; €1.99 vs €0.41 with the higher cap), even though disclosure raises everyone’s pay. Silence free-rides on a colleague’s honesty, and hiding worsens over the rounds. The results page holds the full figures; the model page holds the theory and an interactive equilibrium explorer.

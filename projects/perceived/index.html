Play the estimation task

A grid of 100 dots, each red or black. The task is simply to judge how many are red.

Your guess

You see the grid for exactly 5 seconds, then it is hidden and you enter your estimate.

The viewing time is fixed at 5 seconds for everyone, so working harder or staring longer cannot buy you more accuracy. That is the whole point of the design.

Your advice

A client has already made a first guess on a grid you get to view for 6 seconds. You decide whether to send advice, and if so, what revised red count to recommend.

Client side The fee does move the client (exploratory).

Away from the pre-registered advisor tests, the fee leaves clear traces on the client’s behaviour. These effects are exploratory, not confirmatory.

Comply more
Clients follow expensive advice more closely, moving further towards a recommendation when they have paid the high fee for it.
Reward activity
Clients prefer advisors who advise more often, rating and retaining frequent advisers more favourably.
Punish big moves
Clients sanction advisors who suggest large changes from the status quo, penalising bold departures from their first guess.
An asymmetry. Clients respond to the fee, complying more, rewarding activity and punishing bold moves, but advisors do not. The price paid changes the buyer’s behaviour while leaving the seller’s advice untouched.

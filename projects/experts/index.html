Experts and Dispersed Information

Error rates and confidence in expert versus novice judgement

Julian Tait
All projects
Experts and Dispersed Information
Simulator Game
Hypothesis Confidence and accuracy should come apart.
  • Same task for everyone: judge whether a scatter plot slopes up or down — the expert sees 80 points, each novice only 4.
  • People judge by how striking a pattern looks, not by how much data sits behind it (Griffin & Tversky).
  • Four dots often look like a clear trend — novices expected to be confidently wrong (misreading roughly 4 in 10).
  • The expert’s trend hides in a cloud of 80 points — right ~95% of the time, yet underconfident.
  • Groups pooling these judgements expected to under-weight their hidden expert.
Results The simulator makes the divergence visible, dot by dot.

With the canonical two-state design (a slope of 0 or +0.2), the simulation puts the novice’s error rate around 31% against the expert’s few percent, and the sampling distributions show why: the novice’s estimate is diffuse where the expert’s is sharp. The group panels then compare precision weighting (the optimal rule) with equal and behavioural weighting, making visible how much accuracy a group loses when it fails to find its expert. The simulator lets you rerun every panel under your own design; the game lets you feel the novice’s overconfidence yourself.

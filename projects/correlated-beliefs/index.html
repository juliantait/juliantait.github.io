The Unprotected Majority
Overview Stimuli Model
Hypothesis Identity inference can silence the majority.

Voicing an opinion does more than express a view. When traits are spread differently across groups, a listener can infer the speaker’s group from what they say, and speakers who anticipate that inference face a cost of speaking. The model predicts silence wherever this inference cost outweighs the expressive value of the view. Crucially, the cost depends on how group-revealing an opinion is, not on how many people hold it, so we expected even majority-held opinions to fall silent wherever they are the more revealing ones: an unprotected majority.

Results The revealing view goes quiet, even when most people hold it.

Under the canonical calibration the model delivers exactly this pattern. At the majority preset (65% of group A and 95% of group B hold trait 1), 28% of group A stay silent against 19% of group B, even though the silenced view is held by a majority overall. Silence itself becomes informative in equilibrium: observing it pushes the posterior that the sender belongs to group A to 0.59. Switching identity inference off removes the asymmetry entirely.
